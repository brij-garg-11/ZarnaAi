The core challenge: Everything right now is hardcoded for Zarna — her voice, her training data, her phone number, her keywords. To offer this to other creators, you need a configurable layer between the engine and each client's identity.

Must-Have Before You Can Sell This

1

Voice & Persona Configuration System

Right now Zarna's style guide, tone rules, and "what to avoid" are baked into the prompt. You need a per-client config that swaps in a different creator's voice, name, brand rules, and reply style without touching code.

  • Creator name, pronouns, brand tone (edgy, warm, dry, etc.)
  • What topics are off-limits for them
  • Signature phrases / emojis they use
  • How they want fans to feel after texting
2

Content Ingestion Onboarding Kit

You already have scripts for YouTube, podcasts, specials, books. Package them into a repeatable onboarding checklist — a client gives you their links, you run the pipeline, embeddings are built. Clients can't go live until this is done.

  • Intake form: YouTube channel, podcast RSS, specials, book PDF, social voice notes
  • A "knowledge health" check — how many chunks exist, are there gaps?
  • Minimum viable corpus: what's the floor before the AI sounds coherent?
3

Per-Client Deployment Template (or Multi-Tenancy)

You have two options: deploy a separate isolated instance per client (simpler, safer), or build one multi-tenant system. For now, separate Railway deployments with a shared template is the right call — less risk, faster to ship.

  • One Railway project template with env vars for: client name, SMS creds, AI config, DB URL
  • A deployment checklist so you can spin up a new client in <1 day
  • Separate databases per client — fan data must not cross
4

Live Demo You Can Text Right Now

The single best sales tool is handing a prospect your phone number and saying "text it." Keep a generic "demo creator" instance live — or use Zarna's live instance as proof. A creator who texts the AI and hears a personality that sounds human is sold.

  • A demo number they can text during your pitch meeting
  • Dashboard screen recording or live walkthrough ready to go

Should Build Soon

5

Client Onboarding Document & Admin Walkthrough

A PDF or Notion doc the client keeps — explains exactly what the dashboard does, what the AI knows, what they can and can't control, and who to contact when something is off.

6

Blast Approval Workflow

Right now blasts require two checkboxes in the dashboard. For liability, you want a paper trail — either a Slack/email approval step, or a "client approves in the dashboard with their own login." This protects you on TCPA exposure.

7

Per-Client Admin Login (not shared password)

Currently one shared admin password. For client deployments, each client needs their own login so you can audit who did what, and they can't accidentally see another client's data if you ever consolidate.

8

Monthly Reporting / Engagement Summary

Clients will want to see numbers even if they don't log in. An auto-generated monthly email or PDF showing subscriber growth, engagement rate, top fan messages, and blast performance keeps them engaged and justifies renewal.

Nice to Have (Differentiation)

9

Voice Refresh Workflow

When a creator drops a new special, podcast, or book — a process to add new content to the knowledge base without breaking what's already there. Right now this is a manual re-ingestion.

10

AI Quality Monitoring Dashboard

A way to flag replies the AI gave that were off-brand, weird, or wrong. Either you review them, or the client can flag a reply in the thread view and you get notified. This is your QA layer at scale.

The pitch in one sentence: "Your fans can text a number and get replies that sound exactly like you — and you can see, understand, and message your audience from a dashboard, without ever touching a phone."

Walk Them Through This, In Order

1

The Fan Experience

Start with what their fans will feel — not what the tech does. Fans text a number. They get replies in the creator's voice, drawn from their real material (specials, podcasts, interviews, books). It feels personal. The AI doesn't pretend to be human — it's a companion that sounds like them.

"Imagine a fan texting you after a show and getting a reply that actually sounds like you answered — instantly, at 2am, to 4,000 people."
2

The Live Show / Stream Feature

This is often the hook for comedians and performers. At a show, fans text a keyword to get on the list. They get a personalized welcome text right away. After the show, you can blast the whole room with tour announcements, merch drops, anything. For live streams, same thing — people watching from home join a list.

"You leave every show with every phone number in the room, and a direct line to all of them forever."
3

The Dashboard — Their Command Center

Show them the admin. Walk through: subscriber count, engagement rate, conversation threads, fan profiles with memory, and the live show blasting tool. Make it feel like having a CRM that actually works for a performer.

"You can see who's texting, what they're asking, where they're from — and message all of them in one click."
4

How It's Trained on Them

Explain that the AI isn't generic — it's trained on their specials, their podcast, their book, their voice. It knows their bits, their stories, their opinions. You feed it their content; it learns how they talk.

"It's not ChatGPT. It knows your last special. It knows the bit you do about your dad. It answers like you would."
5

What You Handle (Everything Technical)

They don't manage hosting, phone numbers, AI APIs, databases, or any of it. You run all of it. They get a product. This is your managed service pitch — what you absorb so they don't have to think about it.

6

What They're Responsible For

Be upfront: they own their SMS opt-in consent records, their privacy policy, and approving any blast before it goes out. You handle the technical side; they handle the legal relationship with their fans. This protects both of you.

7

Show the Numbers (Social Proof from Zarna)

Use the Rhode Island beta data. Real engagement numbers from a real show. 4,500+ subscribers, 16% engagement rate, fans texting back at 2.8 texts per active user. Show them this is not a concept — it's live and working.

8

Timeline to Go Live

Tell them what it takes: they give you their content (YouTube, podcast, specials), you build the knowledge base and configure the voice, you set up SMS, they test it, you go live. Frame a realistic timeline (2–4 weeks) and what you need from them to start.

Mental model: You are the infrastructure owner and technical operator. The client is the brand owner. You give them the steering wheel (dashboard) — you own the engine, the road, and the car.

You (Operator) Control

◆

The codebase and all updates

You ship new features, fix bugs, update the AI models. Client never touches the code.

◆

Hosting and infrastructure (Railway)

Server uptime, scaling, deployments — all yours. Client gets no server access.

◆

Database — all fan data

You hold the fan phone numbers, conversation history, profile memory, and show signup lists. You back it up, secure it, and manage access.

◆

The AI layer (Gemini API)

Model selection, prompt engineering, reply quality, voice configuration, safety rules — all yours. Client doesn't write prompts.

◆

SMS pipeline (Twilio / SlickText accounts)

Phone numbers, webhook routing, delivery, opt-out filtering, STOP handling — you manage the accounts and credentials.

◆

Knowledge base / training data

You ingest the client's content, build embeddings, and control what the AI knows. You also control when and how it gets updated.

◆

Rate limiting and abuse protection

Spam filters, concurrent AI limits, deduplication — you configure and tune these for each deployment.

◆

Security and credentials

API keys, webhook secrets, admin passwords — you rotate and manage all of this.

Client Controls (via Dashboard)

◇

Creating and managing live shows / streams

They set up the event, pick the keyword, set timing, choose the event type. They go live when ready. They end the show. They own the event setup.

◇

Sending bulk blasts to show lists

They write the message, review the sample, check the two confirmation boxes, and hit send. You require their sign-off — this is their content going to their fans.

◇

Viewing fan profiles and conversations

They can see who's texting, what was said, what the AI remembered about a fan. Read access to the audience they built.

◇

Exporting their audience (CSV downloads)

They can download subscriber lists and conversation exports. It's their audience — they should be able to take the list.

◇

Their privacy policy and SMS terms

They publish this on their website. It's their legal relationship with their fans. You don't write it or own it.

◇

Original fan opt-in consent

How fans originally signed up (website, show, social) is on the client. They are responsible for valid consent before those numbers exist in the system.

◇

What content they give you to train on

They choose what specials, podcasts, interviews, and other material go into the knowledge base. That input shapes the AI's personality and accuracy.

The Key Line to Communicate

You run the machine. They drive it.
They get a dashboard that feels like theirs — and it is, in terms of what matters to them (their audience, their events, their blasts). But the infrastructure, the AI, the security, the uptime — that's yours. When something breaks, you fix it. When Gemini updates their pricing, you absorb it. When a new AI model drops that improves quality, you ship it. That's what they're paying for.

Dry? Warm? Self-deprecating? Brutally honest? This seeds the style guide.

✦

Any subjects they don't want the AI touching — politics, ex-partners, anything sensitive.
